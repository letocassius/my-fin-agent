# Financial Basics

## Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

The P/E ratio is one of the most widely used metrics for valuing a stock. It represents how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of earnings.

**Formula:** P/E Ratio = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A high P/E ratio may indicate that the market expects high future growth, or that the stock is overvalued. A low P/E may indicate undervaluation or weak growth prospects.

- **Trailing P/E:** Uses actual earnings from the past 12 months
- **Forward P/E:** Uses projected earnings for the next 12 months
- **Sector context matters:** Tech companies typically have higher P/E ratios than utilities

Industry average P/E ratios vary significantly. The S&P 500 historical average P/E is approximately 15–20.

---

## Earnings Per Share (EPS)

EPS measures how much net income a company earns per outstanding share of common stock.

**Formula:** EPS = (Net Income - Preferred Dividends) / Weighted Average Shares Outstanding

- **Basic EPS:** Uses basic share count
- **Diluted EPS:** Accounts for all potential shares (options, warrants, convertibles) — more conservative
- Higher EPS generally indicates greater profitability
- EPS growth over time signals improving performance

## Revenue vs. Net Income

**Revenue** (also called "top line") is the total amount of money a company generates from its core business activities before any expenses are deducted. It is the starting point of the income statement.

**Net Income** (also called "bottom line" or "profit") is what remains after deducting all expenses from revenue, including:
- Cost of goods sold (COGS)
- Operating expenses (salaries, rent, marketing)
- Depreciation and amortization
- Interest expense
- Taxes

**Key relationship:** Revenue - All Expenses = Net Income

A company can have high revenue but low or negative net income if its costs are high (e.g., early-stage growth companies). Profitability requires net income to be positive.

**Gross Profit** = Revenue - COGS (before operating expenses)

**Operating Income (EBIT)** = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses

## Market Capitalization

Market cap is the total market value of a company's outstanding shares.

**Formula:** Market Cap = Share Price × Total Shares Outstanding

**Size categories:**
- **Mega-cap:** > $200 billion
- **Large-cap:** $10–200 billion
- **Mid-cap:** $2–10 billion
- **Small-cap:** $300 million–$2 billion
- **Micro-cap:** < $300 million

Market cap reflects investor perception of a company's total worth, not its book value or assets.

---

## Dividends

Dividends are distributions of a company's earnings to shareholders, typically paid quarterly.

**Dividend Yield** = Annual Dividend Per Share / Stock Price × 100%

**Dividend Payout Ratio** = Dividends Paid / Net Income

- Companies with stable, mature businesses (utilities, consumer staples) tend to pay higher dividends
- Growth companies often reinvest earnings rather than pay dividends
- A rising dividend over time signals financial health
- A very high yield can signal the stock price has fallen sharply (a warning sign)

**Ex-Dividend Date:** You must own the stock before this date to receive the next dividend payment.

---

## Book Value and Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

**Book Value** = Total Assets - Total Liabilities (shareholders' equity)

**P/B Ratio** = Stock Price / Book Value Per Share

A P/B ratio below 1 may indicate undervaluation. Financial companies (banks, insurance) are frequently valued using P/B.

---

## Enterprise Value (EV)

Enterprise Value is a comprehensive measure of a company's total value, including debt.

**Formula:** EV = Market Cap + Total Debt - Cash and Cash Equivalents

**EV/EBITDA** is a commonly used valuation multiple that accounts for capital structure differences between companies.
