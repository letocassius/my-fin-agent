# Market Concepts

## Bull and Bear Markets

### Bull Market
A period of sustained rising prices, typically defined as a 20%+ gain from a recent low. Characterized by:
- Strong investor confidence
- Economic expansion
- Rising corporate earnings
- Low unemployment
- The longest U.S. bull market ran from 2009 to 2020 (COVID crash)

### Bear Market
A period of sustained falling prices, typically defined as a 20%+ decline from a recent high. Characterized by:
- Pessimism and risk aversion
- Economic contraction or recession fears
- Declining corporate earnings
- Rising unemployment
- Bear markets on average last 9–10 months; bull markets average 2.7 years

### Market Correction
A decline of 10–20% from a recent peak. Less severe than a bear market. Corrections are normal and occur roughly once per year historically.

## Volatility

Volatility measures the degree of price fluctuation in a security or market.

### Historical Volatility
Calculated from actual past price movements (typically using standard deviation of returns).

### Implied Volatility (IV)
Derived from options prices; reflects the market's expectation of future volatility. High IV = expensive options = market expects large moves.

### VIX (CBOE Volatility Index)
Known as the "fear gauge," the VIX measures expected 30-day volatility of the S&P 500.
- VIX < 15: Low volatility, calm markets
- VIX 15–25: Moderate volatility, normal conditions
- VIX > 25: Elevated fear, significant uncertainty
- VIX > 40: Extreme fear (seen during 2008 crisis, COVID crash)

## Sector Rotation

Sector rotation is the flow of investment capital from one industry sector to another as economic conditions change. Different sectors tend to outperform at different stages of the economic cycle.

### Economic Cycle and Typical Sector Performance

**Early Expansion:**
- Outperformers: Consumer Discretionary, Financials, Industrials
- Investors bet on economic recovery, credit expansion

**Mid Expansion:**
- Outperformers: Information Technology, Communication Services
- Corporate spending increases, earnings accelerate

**Late Expansion:**
- Outperformers: Energy, Materials, Industrials
- Inflation rises, commodity demand peaks

**Recession:**
- Outperformers: Consumer Staples, Healthcare, Utilities
- Defensive sectors with stable earnings regardless of economic conditions

## Market Indices

### S&P 500
The most widely followed U.S. equity index. Tracks 500 large-cap U.S. companies, market-cap weighted. Broadly considered the benchmark for U.S. stock market performance.

### Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)
Tracks 30 large U.S. blue-chip companies. Price-weighted (not market-cap weighted). Less representative than the S&P 500 but widely reported.

### Nasdaq Composite
Tracks all ~3,000+ stocks listed on the Nasdaq exchange. Heavily weighted toward technology and growth companies.

### Russell 2000
Tracks 2,000 small-cap U.S. stocks. Used as a benchmark for small-cap performance.

## Market Microstructure

### Bid-Ask Spread
- **Bid:** The highest price a buyer is willing to pay
- **Ask (Offer):** The lowest price a seller is willing to accept
- **Spread:** The difference between bid and ask; represents a transaction cost
- Tighter spreads = more liquid securities

### Market Orders vs. Limit Orders
- **Market Order:** Execute immediately at the best available price
- **Limit Order:** Execute only at the specified price or better

### Liquidity
How easily an asset can be bought or sold without significantly moving its price. High-volume, large-cap stocks have high liquidity. Small-cap, thinly-traded stocks have low liquidity.

## Short Selling

Short selling is the practice of borrowing shares and selling them with the intent to repurchase them at a lower price.

**Process:**
1. Borrow shares from a broker
2. Sell them at current market price
3. Later, buy back at (hopefully) a lower price
4. Return shares to lender; keep the difference as profit

**Risk:** Unlike buying stocks (maximum loss = investment), short sellers face theoretically unlimited losses if the stock price rises.

**Short Interest:** The percentage of shares sold short. High short interest can lead to a "short squeeze" if the price rises — forced buybacks accelerate the rally.

## Monetary Policy and Markets

### Federal Reserve (Fed) and Interest Rates
The Fed sets the federal funds rate, which influences borrowing costs throughout the economy.

**Rate hikes (tightening):**
- Make borrowing more expensive
- Reduce consumer and business spending
- Typically bearish for growth stocks (higher discount rate reduces present value of future earnings)
- Typically positive for financials

**Rate cuts (easing):**
- Stimulate borrowing and spending
- Typically bullish for equities, especially growth stocks
- Typically negative for the U.S. dollar

### Yield Curve
The yield curve plots interest rates of bonds with equal credit quality but different maturities.
- **Normal yield curve:** Long-term rates > short-term rates (healthy)
- **Inverted yield curve:** Short-term rates > long-term rates — historically a recession predictor
- **Flat yield curve:** Little difference between short and long-term rates

## Inflation and Its Impact on Markets

**CPI (Consumer Price Index):** Measures the average change in prices paid by consumers.

**Impact of high inflation:**
- Erodes purchasing power
- Fed raises rates to combat → higher borrowing costs
- Negative for long-duration bonds and growth stocks
- Positive for commodities, energy, real assets

**Impact of low inflation / deflation:**
- Can signal weak economic demand
- Fed may cut rates to stimulate
- Generally positive for bonds

## Derivatives Overview

### Options
Contracts that give the buyer the right (but not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) an asset at a specified price (strike) before a specified date (expiration).

- **Call option:** Right to buy — profitable when price rises
- **Put option:** Right to sell — profitable when price falls
- **Premium:** Price paid for the option

### Futures
Contracts obligating the buyer to purchase an asset at a predetermined price and date. Widely used in commodities and for hedging.

## P/E Ratio

**Price ÷ Earnings per Share** — how much investors pay per $1 of profit.

| P/E | Signal |
|-----|--------|
| High | Growth expected, or overvalued |
| Low | Slow growth expected, or undervalued |
| Negative | Company losing money — ratio meaningless |

**Variants:** Trailing (past 12mo EPS) vs. Forward (estimated EPS).
Always compare P/E to industry peers or historical averages — never in isolation.
